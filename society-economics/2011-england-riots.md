# 2011 England Riots

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Mark Duggan (victim of police shooting, catalyzing figure), Tariq Jahan (father of riot casualty Haroon Jahan; public symbol of reconciliation), Theresa May (Home Secretary), David Cameron (Prime Minister), Boris Johnson (Mayor of London), Sir Hugh Orde (ACPO president), Bernard Hogan-Howe (Metropolitan Police Commissioner-designate)

## Summary

Between August 6 and 11, 2011, England experienced its most widespread urban unrest in a generation. Starting in Tottenham, North London, following the police killing of Mark Duggan on August 4, the riots spread rapidly across London and then to Birmingham, Manchester, Salford, Nottingham, Liverpool, Bristol, Coventry, and more than 20 other towns. By the time order was restored, five people had died, over 200 were injured (the majority police officers), more than 3,100 people had been arrested, approximately 2,815 homes and businesses were damaged or destroyed, and insured property losses were estimated at **£200 million** — with total economic impact, including lost business and emergency services costs, assessed at closer to £500 million. The riots were simultaneously an expression of social grievance, an outbreak of opportunistic criminality, and a demonstration of technology's double-edged role in modern civil unrest.

## Background: Mark Duggan and the Tottenham Shooting

Mark Duggan, born in 1981 and raised in Tottenham, was 29 years old at the time of his death. He had prior criminal convictions and was known to police as a figure in North London gang activity. On August 4, 2011, officers from Operation Trident — a Metropolitan Police unit specializing in gun crime in Black communities — stopped the minicab in which Duggan was traveling on Tottenham Hale Retail Park Road, acting on intelligence that he was carrying a firearm. During the stop, Duggan was shot twice by a specialist firearms officer and died at the scene. The gun he had reportedly been carrying — a converted BBM blank-firing pistol loaded with live rounds — was found approximately 10–14 feet from his body, in grass on the other side of a fence; this detail later became central to an inquest into whether he had been shot while armed.

An IPCC (Independent Police Complaints Commission) referral was immediately initiated. On August 6, family members and community leaders gathered at Tottenham police station seeking information from the Met; the family waited for hours without receiving a meeting with a senior officer. By evening, what began as a peaceful vigil had escalated — specific accounts of what triggered the violence differ — into clashes, and within hours, buildings on Tottenham High Road were burning, including a Carpetright store and several residential properties above shops. Forty-nine police officers were injured on that first night.

## The Birmingham Hit-and-Run and Tariq Jahan

One of the most tragic incidents of the riots occurred in the Winson Green area of Birmingham in the early hours of August 10. Haroon Jahan (20), Shahzad Ali (30), and Abdul Musavir (31) were part of a community group watching over local businesses when a car ploughed through the group, killing all three. The driver, Afzal Nawaz, was convicted of murder. Haroon's father, Tariq Jahan, gave an extraordinary impromptu press conference hours after learning of his son's death, calling on young men of all backgrounds to stop the violence: "I lost my son. Step forward if you want to lose your sons. Otherwise, calm down and go home." His words became internationally reported; President Obama praised him and he was widely seen as the human embodiment of the community spirit that ultimately reasserted itself against the chaos.

## Technology's Dual Role

BlackBerry Messenger (BBM) emerged as a primary tool for rioters to organize, primarily because its encrypted group messaging was harder for police to monitor than Twitter or Facebook. On August 8, Research In Motion (BlackBerry's maker) issued a statement offering to cooperate with authorities — prompting a debate about device encryption and law enforcement access that prefigured later controversies. Simultaneously, Twitter and Facebook facilitated rapid, high-visibility community responses: the hashtag #riotcleanup emerged on the evening of August 9 and by the morning of August 10 thousands of volunteers — quickly dubbed "riot wombles" — had gathered with brooms and dustpans at riot sites in Clapham, Hackney, Croydon, and Ealing. Crowdsourced sites documenting looters and identifying stolen goods assisted police investigations. The duality of social media in the riots informed subsequent policy debates about internet regulation during civil emergencies.

## Government Response

Prime Minister David Cameron cut short his holiday in Tuscany and returned on August 9. On August 10, parliament was recalled from summer recess — an emergency session not triggered by an international crisis but by domestic disorder. Cameron told the House of Commons that 16,000 police were deployed in London that night (up from the 6,000 fielded on the worst night of riots). He promised to use "every tool available" to restore order, including water cannon and, if necessary, baton rounds (plastic bullets). Mayor Boris Johnson, who had also been on holiday, returned to London under significant public criticism for his delayed response. Home Secretary Theresa May chaired emergency COBRA meetings coordinating the multi-agency response; the operation to police the London riots was codenamed **Operation Withern**, coordinated by the Metropolitan Police.

ACPO president Sir Hugh Orde publicly criticized any suggestion of using rubber bullets on English streets, arguing the tactics were unsuitable for the type of disorder being experienced. The Metropolitan Police, whose initial response on August 6–7 was widely criticized as understrength and passive, substantially reinforced its numbers from August 8 onward.

## Judicial Aftermath

Fast-tracked courts sat through the nights of August 9–11, with magistrates hearing emergency bail applications and initial hearings. Sentencing guidelines were notably harsher than in comparable peacetime offences:

- A man who stole £3.50 worth of water from a looted Lidl supermarket was jailed for six months.
- Two men in Manchester who posted Facebook messages encouraging riots — the events they were calling for did not take place — were jailed for four years each; their convictions were upheld on appeal but sentences reduced to 15 months.
- By 2012, approximately 4,000 people had been processed through the courts in relation to the riots.
- The **Riots Communities and Victims Panel**, set up by the government in 2011, found that 85% of those convicted had prior criminal records, and that many had experienced poverty, educational failure, and social exclusion.

## Root Causes Debate

The causes of the riots were contested along sharp political lines. A broad-based academic and civic analysis emphasized structural factors:

- **Unemployment**: UK youth unemployment stood at approximately 21% in mid-2011 — the highest since comparable records began. In the most affected boroughs of Hackney, Haringey, and Lambeth, youth unemployment exceeded 30%.
- **Austerity**: The Coalition government's spending cuts (announced in the October 2010 Comprehensive Spending Review) included elimination of the Education Maintenance Allowance (EMA) for post-16 students and cuts to local authority youth services — programmes disproportionately used in deprived urban communities.
- **Police-community relations**: Decades of tension between the Metropolitan Police and Black communities in particular, rooted in the 1981 Brixton riots and the use of stop-and-search powers, had not been resolved. The Duggan shooting, in a context of longstanding community distrust, provided the immediate trigger.

Conservative commentators emphasized criminality, family breakdown, gang culture, and moral collapse. Cameron's post-riot speech described a "moral collapse" in sections of society. This framing was vigorously disputed by sociologists, community advocates, and the Riots Panel's own findings, which found that only 13% of those convicted were gang members.

## Significance

The 2011 England Riots forced a national reckoning with the cumulative effects of inequality, austerity, policing failures, and social fragmentation. The riots did not produce sustained political reform: several subsequent policy reviews, including the Riots Panel's 2012 final report, produced recommendations that were largely shelved or implemented only partially. Theresa May's 2014 Manchester speech acknowledging that "stop and search" was used in a discriminatory and counterproductive manner drew on lessons identified partly by the riots. The riots also permanently altered public and policy discourse about social media regulation, with the government briefly exploring — then abandoning — the idea of being able to switch off social media platforms during civil emergencies. The community resilience demonstrated by the "riot wombles," Tariq Jahan's address, and hundreds of local restoration efforts became a parallel narrative to the destruction — a story about the persistence of civic solidarity under pressure.
