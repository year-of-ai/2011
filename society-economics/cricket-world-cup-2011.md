# Cricket World Cup 2011

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Yuvraj Singh, Sachin Tendulkar, MS Dhoni, Mahela Jayawardene, Tillakaratne Dilshan, Zaheer Khan, Shahid Afridi

## Summary

The ICC Cricket World Cup 2011 took place from February 19 to April 2, 2011, co-hosted across India, Sri Lanka, and Bangladesh—the first tournament to include Bangladesh as a co-host nation. Fourteen teams competed in a round-robin group stage followed by knockout rounds. India defeated Sri Lanka by six wickets in the final at the Wankhede Stadium in Mumbai on April 2, 2011, becoming the first nation to win the Cricket World Cup on home soil and the first World Cup final between two Asian nations.

India's 28-year drought since their 1983 triumph ended dramatically: chasing Sri Lanka's total of 274 for 6, India reached 277 for 4 in 48.2 overs. Captain MS Dhoni promoted himself up the batting order to number five, scored an unbeaten 91 off 79 balls, and hit the winning six over long-on—a shot replayed millions of times in Indian broadcast history. The tournament drew 1,229,826 spectators across 49 matches.

Yuvraj Singh of India earned the Player of the Tournament award after scoring 362 runs at an average of 90.50 and taking 15 wickets, making him only the third player to achieve a double of 300+ runs and 15+ wickets in a single World Cup. It was later revealed that Yuvraj played the entire tournament while suffering from a malignant lung tumor; he was diagnosed with cancer in August 2011 and underwent chemotherapy before returning to international cricket in 2012.

## Tournament Format and Venues

The 10th edition of the ICC World Cup was hosted at 14 venues across the three co-host nations:

- **India** (9 venues): Eden Gardens (Kolkata), Wankhede Stadium (Mumbai), M. Chinnaswamy Stadium (Bengaluru), MA Chidambaram Stadium (Chennai), Punjab Cricket Association Stadium (Mohali), Vidarbha Cricket Association Stadium (Nagpur), Holkar Cricket Stadium (Indore), Sardar Patel Stadium (Ahmedabad), Green Park (Kanpur)
- **Sri Lanka** (3 venues): R. Premadasa Stadium (Colombo), Pallekele International Cricket Stadium, Mahinda Rajapaksa International Cricket Stadium (Hambantota)
- **Bangladesh** (2 venues): Shere Bangla National Stadium (Dhaka), Zohur Ahmed Chowdhury Stadium (Chittagong)

Pakistan was originally named as a co-host but was removed by the ICC in 2009 following the Lahore attack on the Sri Lanka cricket team bus in March 2009, which killed eight people and wounded seven players. Bangladesh replaced Pakistan as the fourth co-host. The tournament was reduced from 16 teams (2007 format) to 14, eliminating the preliminary round.

## The Final: April 2, 2011

The final between India and Sri Lanka, played before 33,000 spectators at Wankhede Stadium under floodlights, was the first day-night World Cup final. Sri Lanka batted first and posted 274 for 6 in 50 overs:

- Mahela Jayawardene made an unbeaten 103 off 88 balls—the first century in a World Cup final since Ricky Ponting's 140 for Australia in 2003—anchoring the innings after Sri Lanka lost early wickets.
- Kumar Sangakkara contributed 48 runs before being dismissed.
- India's Zaheer Khan conceded 60 runs but took 2 wickets.

India's run chase initially faltered: openers Virender Sehwag (0) and Sachin Tendulkar (18) fell cheaply, leaving India at 31 for 2. Gautam Gambhir steadied the innings with 97 off 122 balls before being dismissed 3 runs short of a century. Dhoni's match-winning 91*, supported by Yuvraj Singh (21*), carried India home by six wickets with 10 balls to spare.

Sachin Tendulkar's participation in his sixth consecutive World Cup (1992, 1996, 1999, 2003, 2007, 2011) equaled the record then held by Pakistan's Javed Miandad. Tendulkar was India's leading run-scorer of the tournament with 482 runs at an average of 53.55 (second only to Dilshan), though he was dismissed for 18 in the final. India's victory was emotionally framed as a gift to Tendulkar, who had played for 22 years without winning a World Cup; teammates carried him on their shoulders around the Wankhede field in the post-match celebrations.

## Economic and Cultural Impact

The tournament generated an estimated $2 billion in television rights revenue globally, with the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI) and its media partner Star Sports commanding the largest share. The ICC distributed $10 million in prize money, with India receiving $3 million as champions and Sri Lanka $1.5 million as runners-up.

India's World Cup victory prompted mass public celebrations comparable in scale to events triggered by major political milestones. An estimated 100 million people watched the final on Indian television—one of the largest single-event audiences in broadcast history at the time. India's Prime Minister Manmohan Singh hosted the victorious squad at 7 Race Course Road in New Delhi.

The tournament elevated cricket's economic and soft-power weight in South Asia at a time when the [eurozone debt crisis](eurozone-debt-crisis.md) was reshaping Western economic influence globally, underscoring the shift of major economic activity and cultural capital toward Asia. With [world population reaching seven billion](world-population-seven-billion.md) in October 2011 and the fastest demographic growth concentrated in South Asia, the World Cup's 1.2 billion-strong viewership base in India alone illustrated the scale of influence shifting toward the region.

## Significance

India's 2011 World Cup victory was a defining moment in cricket's evolving geopolitical economy. The BCCI's financial dominance—already consolidating through the Indian Premier League (IPL), launched in 2008—was cemented by the tournament's commercial success, firmly establishing India as the de facto governing power of international cricket. The tournament demonstrated that ODI cricket, after concerns about its popularity in the face of the Twenty20 (T20) format, could still generate massive global engagement.

For Sri Lanka, the runner-up finish capped a generation of exceptional batting talent around the core of Jayawardene and Sangakkara, who would retire from ODIs following the 2015 World Cup. Pakistan's early exit (quarter-final loss to India in Mohali) intensified the cricket-nationalism nexus between the two countries: the semi-final between India and Pakistan, played on March 30, 2011 in Mohali, was attended by both nations' Prime Ministers—Manmohan Singh and Yousaf Raza Gillani—in a diplomatic gesture widely covered as "cricket diplomacy."
