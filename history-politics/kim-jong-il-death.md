# Death of Kim Jong Il

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Kim Jong Il (deceased), Kim Jong Un (successor), Jang Sung-taek (regent figure and uncle by marriage), Kim Ki-nam (propaganda chief), Choe Ryong-hae (Korean People's Army political chief), Ri Yong-ho (chief of staff)

## Summary

Kim Jong Il, the supreme leader of North Korea since 1994, died on December 17, 2011, from a massive myocardial infarction (heart attack) while traveling aboard his private train near Pyongyang. Official North Korean accounts stated the attack was brought on by "overwork" and "great mental and physical strain" from field guidance inspections — a characterization outside observers read as consistent with the regime's mythologizing of the leader's dedication to the state. State media withheld the announcement for 51 hours, broadcasting the news only on December 19 through a tearful live television address by news anchor Ri Chun-hee. During that silence, North Korea's borders were sealed and military forces placed on high readiness — evidence of internal deliberations over the political transition.

Kim Jong Un, then believed to be 28 or 29 years old (his exact birth year is disputed between 1983 and 1984), was immediately designated "great successor to the revolutionary cause of Juche." A 10-day state mourning period was decreed, during which all public entertainment was suspended, alcohol sales banned, and citizens required to demonstrate visible grief. Foreign journalists reported that North Koreans who failed to mourn publicly were at risk of punishment. The state funeral was held on December 28, 2011, at the Kumsusan Memorial Palace in Pyongyang, where Kim Jong Il's embalmed body lay in state — alongside that of his father Kim Il Sung, who died in 1994 and whose preserved body has been displayed in the same palace. Kim Jong Un stood at the front of the funeral procession through Pyongyang's streets in temperatures reaching −20 °C, flanked by senior party and military officials.

## Background: Kim Jong Il's Final Years

Kim Jong Il had reportedly suffered a significant stroke in August 2008 that left him visibly diminished; photographs from late 2008 onward showed a noticeably thinner and frailer leader. From that point, the North Korean leadership began accelerating succession planning. At the Third Conference of the Korean Workers' Party (September 2010), Kim Jong Un was publicly named as a four-star general and appointed to the Central Military Commission — his first confirmed public role. This formalized what insiders had known for at least a year: Kim Jong Un was the chosen heir among Kim Jong Il's three sons (the eldest, Kim Jong Nam, had fallen from favor in 2001 after a failed attempt to enter Japan on a forged passport; the middle son, Kim Jong Chul, was reportedly considered too soft).

## The 51-Hour Delay

Analysts widely interpreted the delay between Kim Jong Il's death on December 17 and the public announcement on December 19 at 12:00 noon Pyongyang time as time used to arrange political and military allegiances. Senior party, military, and security figures are believed to have been convened to affirm loyalty to Kim Jong Un before the news could trigger any challenge. The delay also gave time to organize the massive state mourning apparatus and ensure that Jang Sung-taek — Kim Jong Un's uncle by marriage and the regime's most experienced political operator — was in position as a stabilizing mentor figure. International intelligence agencies, including South Korea's NIS (National Intelligence Service), were caught off guard by the announcement; Seoul's initial reports indicating Kim Jong Il might be dead were officially denied by South Korean authorities before the confirmation.

## Funeral and the Succession Tableau

The December 28 funeral was choreographed to project Kim Jong Un's authority. He walked alongside the hearse in a procession along Kim Il Sung Square, with six senior officials — Choe Ryong-hae, Kim Ki-nam, Ri Yong-ho, Jang Sung-taek, Choe Thae-bok, and Kim Yong-chun — positioned as pallbearers alongside the vehicle, symbolically framing the young leader as equal among the regime's most powerful men. The composition of that grouping was read by analysts as a signal of the military-party power balance. On December 30, just 13 days after his father's death, Kim Jong Un was named Supreme Commander of the Korean People's Army, consolidating civilian and military authority at exceptional speed for a leader with no prior executive experience. Within two years, most of the officials who had flanked him at the funeral — including Jang Sung-taek (executed December 2013) and Ri Yong-ho (purged July 2012) — would be removed or killed as Kim Jong Un eliminated potential rivals.

## International Reactions

Responses divided sharply along geopolitical lines:

- **China**: President Hu Jintao, Premier Wen Jiabao, and National People's Congress Chairman Wu Bangguo all visited the North Korean embassy in Beijing on December 20 to offer formal condolences — an unusually high-level display of solidarity, reflecting China's strategic interest in a stable North Korean state. China later sent a delegation to the funeral.
- **South Korea**: President Lee Myung-bak convened the National Security Council within hours of the announcement and placed the military on high alert. Seoul declined to send official condolences, citing "difficult relations" following the 2010 sinking of the corvette *Cheonan* and the Yeonpyeong Island shelling. Families of South Koreans kidnapped by North Korea were permitted to send private condolences, generating domestic controversy.
- **United States**: The Obama administration issued a measured statement expressing hopes for stability and a focus on the Korean people's well-being. Washington did not offer formal condolences.
- **Japan**: Prime Minister Yoshihiko Noda offered no official condolences, and Tokyo heightened intelligence surveillance of North Korea's military posture.
- **Financial markets**: Asian stock markets fell sharply on December 19 — South Korea's KOSPI dropped 3.4%, Japan's Nikkei fell 1.2%, and the South Korean won weakened against the dollar — reflecting investor anxiety about regional instability.

## Significance

Kim Jong Il's death and the succession of Kim Jong Un represented a generational inflection point in one of the world's most opaque and militarized states. No communist hereditary transfer of this scale had occurred since the Soviet and Eastern Bloc collapses; North Korea's smooth succession — or at least its appearance of smoothness — demonstrated the regime's organizational resilience. Kim Jong Un's early consolidation, despite initial Western skepticism that he could maintain control, resulted in a harsher, more centralized administration than his father's: North Korea conducted its first successful satellite launch in December 2012, its third and fourth nuclear tests in 2013 and 2016, and accelerated its intercontinental ballistic missile program. The death of Kim Jong Il thus initiated not a collapse but an intensification of the regime's strategic ambitions, reshaping the security environment of Northeast Asia throughout the 2010s.
