# End of the Iraq War — U.S. Withdrawal

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: President Barack Obama, Defense Secretary Leon Panetta, Iraqi Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki, U.S. Forces–Iraq

## Summary

On December 18, 2011, the last convoy of United States combat troops crossed the border from Iraq into Kuwait, formally ending nearly nine years of American military presence that had begun with the invasion of March 2003. The withdrawal closed a war that toppled the regime of Saddam Hussein, cost the lives of nearly 4,500 American service members and well over a hundred thousand Iraqis, and reshaped the politics of the Middle East. Three days earlier, on December 15, U.S. and Iraqi officials had marked the end of the mission with a casing-of-the-colours ceremony in Baghdad.

The departure was carried out under the terms of a Status of Forces Agreement negotiated in 2008, which set December 31, 2011 as the deadline for a complete withdrawal of American forces from Iraqi territory. Talks on keeping a residual training force collapsed over the issue of legal immunity for U.S. personnel, and President Barack Obama announced in October 2011 that all troops would come home by year's end.

## Background

The United States invaded Iraq on March 20, 2003, citing the alleged existence of weapons of mass destruction and links to terrorism; no significant stockpiles of such weapons were found. Saddam Hussein's government fell within weeks, but the occupation gave way to a prolonged insurgency, sectarian civil conflict between Sunni and Shia communities, and the rise of al-Qaeda in Iraq. American troop levels peaked at roughly 170,000 during the 2007 "surge" intended to suppress the violence, operating from hundreds of bases across the country.

The legal framework for ending the war was set by the U.S.–Iraq Status of Forces Agreement, signed in 2008 under President George W. Bush, which committed American forces to withdraw from Iraqi cities by mid-2009 and from the country entirely by the end of 2011. Negotiations in 2011 to retain several thousand troops as trainers and advisers foundered when the Iraqi parliament would not grant U.S. soldiers immunity from Iraqi prosecution.

## The Withdrawal

Through the second half of 2011, U.S. Forces–Iraq drew down personnel, closed or transferred bases to Iraqi control, and moved equipment south toward Kuwait. On December 14, 2011, President Obama addressed returning troops at Fort Bragg, North Carolina, declaring that the United States was leaving behind "a sovereign, stable and self-reliant Iraq." On December 15, Defense Secretary Leon Panetta presided over a ceremony in Baghdad that cased the colours of U.S. Forces–Iraq, formally ending the mission.

In the early hours of December 18, 2011, the final convoy — roughly 500 soldiers in a column of armoured vehicles — drove out of Iraq across the southern border into Kuwait, completing the withdrawal ahead of the treaty deadline. A diminished American presence remained at the large U.S. Embassy in Baghdad, protected by private security contractors and supported by a security-cooperation office handling arms sales and training.

## Cost and Aftermath

The Iraq War killed nearly 4,500 U.S. service members and wounded more than 30,000, while estimates of Iraqi deaths ranged from well over one hundred thousand to several times that figure, depending on methodology. The financial cost to the United States ran into the hundreds of billions of dollars, with long-term costs including veterans' care pushing estimates far higher.

The Iraq that American forces left in 2011 remained fragile. Sectarian tensions persisted under the Shia-led government of Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki, and the political settlement proved unstable. Within three years, the Sunni militant group that became the Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant (ISIL) seized large areas of northern and western Iraq, prompting the return of U.S. forces in an advisory and air-support role from 2014. The 2011 withdrawal thus marked not a definitive end to American military involvement in Iraq but the close of its largest phase.

## Significance

The end of the Iraq War was a defining political event of 2011, fulfilling a central promise of Obama's presidency and closing one of the most consequential and contested American military undertakings since the Vietnam War. It reshaped debates over the limits of military intervention, nation-building, and the use of intelligence to justify war. The conflict's human and financial costs, and the instability that followed the withdrawal, informed U.S. caution toward subsequent interventions and remained a touchstone in arguments over American foreign policy for years afterward.
