# WikiLeaks and Bradley Manning

**Category**: History & Politics

**Key figures**: Bradley Manning (U.S. Army intelligence analyst, later Chelsea Manning); Julian Assange (WikiLeaks founder); Adrian Lamo (hacker who reported Manning to authorities); David Leigh and Luke Harding (*The Guardian*, journalists); Harold Koh (U.S. State Department Legal Adviser); Barack Obama (U.S. President)

## Summary

In 2011, the international community continued to grapple with the fallout from WikiLeaks' 2010 publication of the largest trove of classified diplomatic and military documents in American history. On March 1, 2011, the U.S. Army filed 22 superseding charges against U.S. Army Private First Class Bradley Manning — a 23-year-old intelligence analyst held in pre-trial detention since May 2010 — including "aiding the enemy," a capital offense, though prosecutors stated they would not seek the death penalty. The leaked materials — 251,287 U.S. State Department diplomatic cables, roughly 400,000 "Iraq War Logs," 90,000 "Afghan War Diary" field reports, and the "Collateral Murder" video of a 2007 Apache helicopter attack in Baghdad — together constituted an unprecedented mass exposure of government secrecy in the digital age. The case advanced to a pre-trial Article 32 hearing at Fort Meade, Maryland, that opened on December 16, 2011. Meanwhile, WikiLeaks founder Julian Assange spent most of 2011 under house arrest in England fighting extradition to Sweden on unrelated sexual assault allegations, while WikiLeaks itself remained a polarizing global phenomenon.

## Significance

The WikiLeaks disclosures and Manning's prosecution in 2011 posed foundational questions about government transparency, classified information, and the ethics of whistleblowing that remained unresolved through the decade. The cables revealed the gap between official public positions and private diplomatic realities — candid characterizations of foreign leaders, evidence of civilian casualties being under-counted, and the mechanics of U.S. covert influence operations. Some of the exposed cables — particularly those revealing the corruption and repression of Ben Ali's Tunisia — are credited by scholars with accelerating the Arab Spring uprisings that began in December 2010 (see [Arab Spring](arab-spring.md) and [Tunisian Revolution 2011](tunisian-revolution-2011.md)).

The Manning case became a generational fault line. For transparency advocates, Manning was a whistleblower and conscientious objector to war crimes. For the national security establishment, the leaks endangered intelligence sources, compromised diplomatic relationships, and violated oaths of service. The case foreshadowed Edward Snowden's 2013 NSA disclosures and became central to debates about the proper limits of state secrecy, the digital security of classified systems, and the treatment of suspected leakers in pre-trial detention.

## Background: WikiLeaks and Cablegate

WikiLeaks, founded in 2006 by Julian Assange, John Shipton, and others, positioned itself as a platform for anonymous disclosure of classified government and corporate information. Before 2010, it had published materials on Icelandic banking failures, Kenyan government corruption, and U.S. Army Standard Operating Procedures for Camp Delta at Guantanamo Bay.

On April 5, 2010, WikiLeaks released **"Collateral Murder"** — edited and unedited footage from a U.S. Army AH-64 Apache helicopter gun camera showing a July 12, 2007 airstrike in the New Baghdad district of Baghdad, Iraq, that killed approximately 18 people, including two Reuters journalists (Namir Noor-Eldeen and Saeed Chmagh) and an Iraqi adult who arrived in a van to help the wounded. The footage showed flight crew joking during and after the strikes and became one of 2011's most-debated media artifacts.

Beginning November 28, 2010, WikiLeaks began releasing **"Cablegate"** — a cache of 251,287 classified U.S. State Department diplomatic cables (dating from December 1966 to February 2010) in partnership with five major newspapers: *The Guardian*, *The New York Times*, *Der Spiegel*, *Le Monde*, and *El País*. The cables revealed:

- **Political candor**: Characterizations of foreign leaders — Russian PM Vladimir Putin described as an "alpha dog," Italian PM Silvio Berlusconi as having "feckless foreign policy adventures"; Saudi King Abdullah repeatedly urging the U.S. to "cut off the head of the snake" (Iran's nuclear program)
- **Civilian casualties**: Evidence that U.S. military personnel in Iraq and Afghanistan had under-reported civilian deaths to meet Pentagon standards
- **Tunisia**: Cables from Ambassador Robert Godec (2008–2009) describing the Ben Ali regime's systemic corruption, nepotism, and repression in extraordinarily frank terms — helping validate protesters' grievances in the weeks before Ben Ali fled on January 14, 2011
- **Yemen**: President Ali Abdullah Saleh secretly allowing U.S. drone strikes in Yemen while publicly claiming they were Yemeni operations
- **Nuclear concerns**: Pakistani nuclear materials security, Iran's weapons program, North Korean missile technology exports

The scale was without precedent: the U.S. government had never had such a volume of classified diplomatic communications exposed simultaneously to global audiences.

## Bradley Manning: Arrest and Detention (2010–2011)

### Initial Arrest (May 2010)

Bradley Edward Manning, born December 17, 1987, in Crescent, Oklahoma, had enlisted in the U.S. Army in 2007 and was assigned as an intelligence analyst to the 2nd Brigade Combat Team, 10th Mountain Division, at Forward Operating Base Hammer near Baghdad, Iraq. Using his security clearance, he accessed the Secret Internet Protocol Router Network (SIPRNet) and the Joint Worldwide Intelligence Communications System (JWICS) — the classified networks through which much of the leaked material passed.

Manning made contact with WikiLeaks in early 2010 and began downloading classified files. He confided in Adrian Lamo, a known hacker and informant, via online chats in May 2010. Lamo reported Manning to the FBI and U.S. Army Counterintelligence on May 21, 2010. Manning was arrested at FOB Hammer on May 26, 2010.

### Pre-Trial Confinement and Controversy (2010–2011)

Manning was initially held at the Camp Arifjan brig in Kuwait before being transferred in July 2010 to the Marine Corps Brig at Quantico, Virginia. His pre-trial detention conditions became internationally controversial:

- Manning was held in solitary confinement in a 6-by-12-foot cell for approximately 23 hours per day
- He was placed on "suicide watch" for weeks, during which he was required to sleep naked and hand over his glasses
- His possessions and reading materials were severely restricted
- UN Special Rapporteur on Torture Juan Méndez investigated his treatment, ultimately concluding in March 2012 that his conditions amounted to cruel, inhuman, and degrading treatment

Over 250 scholars — including constitutional law scholars from Yale, Harvard, and the University of Pennsylvania — signed an open letter in April 2011 condemning Manning's treatment as a violation of the U.S. Constitution's prohibition against cruel and unusual punishment. P.J. Crowley, U.S. State Department spokesman, resigned on March 13, 2011, after publicly calling the Pentagon's treatment of Manning "ridiculous and counterproductive."

### Charges Filed (March 2011)

On March 1, 2011, the U.S. Army filed 22 superseding charges against Manning, replacing the two initial charges brought in July 2010 after a seven-month investigation. The most serious charge — **aiding the enemy** under Article 104 of the Uniform Code of Military Justice — was a capital offense; the government stated it would not seek the death penalty, but the charge still carried potential life imprisonment. Other charges included:

- Multiple counts of violating the Espionage Act (18 U.S.C. § 793)
- Theft of government property and computer fraud
- Unauthorized transmission of national defense information
- Wrongfully causing intelligence to be published on the internet, knowing it was accessible to the enemy

A pre-trial Article 32 hearing — the military equivalent of a grand jury proceeding — opened at Fort Meade, Maryland, on December 16, 2011, under investigating officer Lieutenant Colonel Paul Almanza, to determine whether the case should proceed to court-martial.

## Julian Assange in 2011

Julian Assange spent 2011 under a form of legal siege. He had been arrested in London on December 7, 2010, on a European Arrest Warrant from Sweden related to sexual assault allegations from two Swedish women. He was released on bail on December 16, 2010, and confined to Ellingham Hall, a country estate in Norfolk, England, wearing an electronic monitoring tag and required to report daily to police. The British courts heard his extradition case throughout 2011, and on November 2, 2011, the Westminster Magistrates' Court ruled he could be extradited to Sweden. Assange appealed, and the UK Supreme Court ultimately upheld extradition in May 2012. (Assange sought and received political asylum from Ecuador in June 2012, remaining in the Ecuadorian Embassy in London for seven years.)

In 2011, Assange remained the public face of WikiLeaks, giving interviews (including an autobiography serialized in *The Guardian* before a dispute over its unauthorized publication), managing the release of cables through media partners, and positioning WikiLeaks as a press freedom organization rather than a criminal enterprise.

## Impact on U.S. Foreign Policy and Digital Security

The Manning disclosures prompted immediate policy changes:

1. **Security compartmentalization**: The U.S. government implemented restrictions on SIPRNet access, cross-domain data transfer, and the use of removable media on classified networks. The "need-to-know" principle had been weakened post-9/11 to encourage information sharing; Manning's case forced a reversal
2. **Diplomatic fallout**: The U.S. conducted a global diplomatic damage-assessment review, with Ambassador John Negroponte coordinating outreach to over 50 allies whose communications were exposed
3. **Free press debate**: The Obama administration investigated whether the New York Times and other newspapers that published leaked cables could be prosecuted under the Espionage Act — ultimately deciding against it
4. **Arab Spring context**: Cables revealing corruption in Tunisia and Yemen contributed to the legitimacy of street protests — a consequence the U.S. government had not anticipated when classifying those diplomatic assessments (see [Arab Spring](arab-spring.md))

The transparency arguments mobilized by WikiLeaks supporters resonated in other 2011 contexts: Occupy Wall Street's demands for financial accountability and democratic transparency (see [Occupy Wall Street](../society-economics/occupy-wall-street.md)) shared ideological ground with the anti-secrecy ethos underlying WikiLeaks, even as the movements operated independently.

## Subsequent Developments (After 2011)

Manning's court-martial concluded on July 30, 2013, when she was convicted on 20 of 22 counts, acquitted on the most serious "aiding the enemy" charge. On August 21, 2013, she was sentenced to 35 years in prison. The following day, August 22, 2013, she announced publicly that she identified as a woman and wished to be known as Chelsea Manning — a disclosure that added a new dimension to public debate about gender identity in the military. President Barack Obama commuted her sentence on January 17, 2017, and she was released from Fort Leavenworth on May 17, 2017.
