# 2011 Norway Attacks

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Anders Behring Breivik (perpetrator), Prime Minister Jens Stoltenberg, the Workers' Youth League (AUF), the 22 July Commission

## Summary

On July 22, 2011, Anders Behring Breivik, a 32-year-old Norwegian far-right extremist, carried out two sequential terrorist attacks that killed 77 people — the deadliest assault on Norwegian soil since the Second World War. He first detonated a large vehicle bomb in the executive government quarter (Regjeringskvartalet) of central Oslo, killing eight people, then drove to the island of Utøya, where the youth wing of the governing Labour Party was holding its annual summer camp, and shot dead 69 more, most of them teenagers, over roughly an hour before surrendering to police.

The attacks were planned over years and accompanied by a 1,500-page manifesto in which Breivik set out an anti-Islam, anti-immigration, and anti-multicultural ideology. The killings shocked Norway, a country with little prior experience of mass political violence, and prompted a national response emphasising openness and democratic resilience rather than security crackdown.

## The Oslo Bombing

At 15:25 Central European Summer Time, a van containing a fertiliser-based bomb of roughly 950 kilograms exploded outside the high-rise housing the office of Prime Minister Jens Stoltenberg and other government ministries. The blast killed eight people, injured more than 200, and shattered windows across the government district. Stoltenberg, who often worked from the building, was not present at the time. In the immediate aftermath, before the perpetrator was identified, some early commentary speculated about Islamist involvement — an assumption that proved entirely mistaken.

## The Utøya Massacre

Less than two hours after the Oslo bombing, Breivik travelled to Utøya, a small island in the Tyrifjorden lake in Buskerud, about 40 kilometres north-west of Oslo. The island was hosting the annual summer camp of the Workers' Youth League (Arbeidernes Ungdomsfylking, or AUF), the youth wing of the ruling Labour Party, attended by several hundred young people. Dressed in a homemade police-style uniform and presenting false identification, Breivik was ferried across to the island, then methodically opened fire on the campers.

Over a period of roughly 72 minutes he killed 69 people and wounded dozens more; many victims were teenagers, and some drowned or were injured trying to swim from the island. Police, hampered by logistical and communication failures including the lack of an immediately available helicopter and boat, reached Utøya more than an hour after the shooting began. Breivik surrendered without resistance when an armed police unit confronted him.

## Perpetrator, Ideology, and Trial

Breivik framed the attacks in a manifesto titled *2083: A European Declaration of Independence*, which he distributed electronically hours before the bombing. It espoused a militant opposition to Islam, immigration, and what he termed "cultural Marxism" and multiculturalism, drawing on the "Eurabia" and Great Replacement conspiracy theories. He described the AUF camp as a legitimate target because, in his view, the Labour Party was responsible for facilitating immigration and multicultural policy.

The question of Breivik's sanity dominated the legal proceedings. An initial court-ordered psychiatric evaluation concluded he was psychotic and criminally insane, while a second evaluation found him sane and criminally responsible. At his 2012 trial, the court ruled him legally sane and convicted him of terrorism and premeditated murder. He received Norway's maximum penalty: 21 years of preventive detention (forvaring), a sentence that can be extended indefinitely in five-year increments for as long as he is judged to pose a danger to society.

## Aftermath and Significance

In the days after the attacks, Prime Minister Stoltenberg responded with a call for "more democracy, more openness, but never naivety," a phrasing that came to symbolise Norway's collective response. Hundreds of thousands of Norwegians took part in memorial gatherings, including "rose marches" in Oslo and other cities. An official inquiry, the 22 July Commission, reported in 2012 that the Oslo bombing might have been prevented and the police response to Utøya was too slow, prompting reforms to Norwegian emergency preparedness, police procedures, and national-security coordination.

The attacks became a reference point in international debates about far-right and lone-actor terrorism, the role of online radicalisation, and the balance between open society and security. They stood in 2011 as a stark counterpoint to that year's wave of pro-democracy movements abroad — a reminder that violent extremism in Europe could arise from domestic, anti-immigrant ideology rather than the external threats that had dominated the prior decade's security thinking.
