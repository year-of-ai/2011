# South Sudan independence

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Salva Kiir Mayardit (first president), John Garang (SPLM/A founder, deceased 2005), Thabo Mbeki (African Union High-Level Panel chair), Omar al-Bashir (Sudan president), Ban Ki-moon (UN Secretary-General)

## Summary

On July 9, 2011, South Sudan became an independent nation after a January 2011 referendum in which 98.83% of southern Sudanese voters chose separation from Sudan, with a voter turnout of 97.58%. The independence came after two devastating civil wars spanning from 1955 to 2005 — the second civil war alone (1983–2005) killed an estimated 2 million people and displaced approximately 4 million more. The referendum was held under the terms of the 2005 Comprehensive Peace Agreement (CPA), signed on January 9, 2005 in Nairobi, Kenya, between the government of Sudan and the Sudan People's Liberation Movement/Army (SPLM/A).

Salva Kiir Mayardit, chairman of the SPLM and governor of South Sudan, was sworn in as the nation's first president on July 9, 2011, in Juba. Sudan formally recognized South Sudan's independence the day before, July 8, 2011. South Sudan was admitted to the United Nations on July 14, 2011, as the 193rd member state, with Ban Ki-moon welcoming it before the General Assembly. The United States, European Union, African Union, and Arab League recognized the new state within days of independence.

## Background: Two Decades of War and the Road to the CPA

The roots of southern Sudanese separatism date to the colonial period. The British administration of Sudan (1899–1956) governed the predominantly African, Christian and animist south separately from the Arab, Muslim north, creating deep cultural and administrative divisions. Fighting began almost immediately after Sudanese independence in 1956, with the First Sudanese Civil War lasting from 1955 to 1972. The Addis Ababa Agreement granted the south regional autonomy, but President Nimeiry's imposition of sharia law in 1983 reignited conflict as the Second Sudanese Civil War.

John Garang de Mabior founded the Sudan People's Liberation Movement/Army (SPLM/A) in 1983, leading the southern resistance movement for over two decades. Garang died in a helicopter crash on July 30, 2005 — just three weeks after signing the CPA — and was succeeded by Salva Kiir Mayardit. International mediation, particularly through the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) and sustained US diplomatic engagement under Presidents Clinton and Bush, was instrumental in brokering the 2005 agreement. The CPA established a six-year interim period and guaranteed the south a self-determination referendum.

## The Referendum: January 9–15, 2011

The independence referendum ran for seven days, from January 9 to January 15, 2011. Voting was monitored by approximately 700 international observers, including delegations from the Carter Center, the African Union, and the United Nations Mission in Sudan (UNMIS). The Southern Sudan Referendum Commission announced official results on February 7, 2011:

- **Votes for secession**: 3,791,709 (98.83%)
- **Votes against secession**: 44,888 (1.17%)
- **Total valid votes**: 3,836,597
- **Voter turnout**: 97.58% of the 3.93 million registered voters

The required threshold for a valid result was 60% voter participation and 50%+1 votes for either option. The margin exceeded all projections. Omar al-Bashir attended the results announcement in Khartoum and accepted the outcome, pledging to respect southern Sudan's choice.

## Independence Day: July 9, 2011

At midnight on July 9, 2011, the flag of the Republic of South Sudan — black, red, and green horizontal stripes with a blue triangle and gold star — was raised in Juba for the first time. The ceremony was attended by dozens of heads of state, including US Secretary of State Hillary Clinton, UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon, and Sudan's Omar al-Bashir. Salva Kiir addressed crowds estimated at over 100,000 in the capital and took the oath of office as president.

South Sudan's population at independence was approximately 8.26 million people, spread across an area of 644,329 square kilometres (larger than France), with Juba as the capital. The country's infrastructure was among the most underdeveloped of any new nation: at independence, South Sudan had only approximately 350 kilometres of paved roads and a literacy rate of roughly 27%.

## Resources, Strategic Position, and Early Challenges

South Sudan's independence was partly shaped by its substantial hydrocarbon reserves. The country holds an estimated 3.5 billion barrels of proven oil reserves, concentrated in the Unity State and Upper Nile State — accounting for roughly 75% of the total Sudanese oil production prior to partition. However, South Sudan's landlocked geography meant its oil had to be exported via pipelines running through Sudan to Port Sudan on the Red Sea, creating immediate dependency on and conflict with its northern neighbour over transit fees and border demarcation.

Border disputes — particularly over the Abyei region, which straddles the border and holds oil reserves — remained unresolved at independence. Fighting erupted in Abyei in May 2011, displacing over 100,000 people, and UN peacekeepers were deployed under UN Security Council Resolution 1990. The absence of a functioning state apparatus, civil society infrastructure, and diversified economy meant South Sudan faced profound governance challenges from day one. An estimated 83% of the population lived below the poverty line at independence, and severe flooding in 2011 displaced hundreds of thousands in Jonglei State.

## Significance

South Sudan's independence was the first successful secession in post-colonial Africa and the first African state to gain independence since Eritrea in 1993. It demonstrated that peaceful, internationally supervised self-determination referenda were achievable even in contexts marked by mass atrocity and decades of warfare. The CPA model — combining ceasefire, power-sharing, and a timed referendum — became a reference point for subsequent peace negotiations on the continent.

Yet the story of South Sudan's independence also illustrates the fragility of new states born from conflict. Civil war resumed in December 2013, when political rivalry between President Kiir and former Vice President Riek Machar triggered ethnically inflected violence that killed tens of thousands and displaced millions. The 2011 moment of hope thus became inseparable from a longer cautionary narrative about the challenges of post-conflict state-building in Africa.
