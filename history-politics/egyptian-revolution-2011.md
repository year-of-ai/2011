# Egyptian Revolution of 2011

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Hosni Mubarak, Omar Suleiman, Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF), Wael Ghonim, Asmaa Mahfouz, April 6 Youth Movement

## Summary

The Egyptian Revolution of 2011 was an 18-day popular uprising that began on January 25, 2011 — a date designated "Police Day" as a national holiday, chosen deliberately by organizers to highlight police brutality — and ended on February 11 when President Hosni Mubarak, who had ruled Egypt for nearly 30 years, resigned and transferred authority to the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF). The revolution was centered on Cairo's Tahrir Square, which became the global symbol of the uprising, though major protests also erupted simultaneously in Alexandria, Suez, Ismailia, and other cities. At least 846 people were killed during the uprising, and more than 6,000 were injured.

The revolution drew on long-standing public grievances: endemic corruption, police brutality, high unemployment (particularly among youth), poverty, and the authoritarian governance entrenched under Mubarak's emergency law, which had been in effect almost continuously since 1981. Inspired by the success of the Tunisian Revolution in January 2011, Egyptian organizers including the April 6 Youth Movement and the "We Are All Khaled Said" Facebook network mobilized tens of thousands into the streets, bypassing state-controlled media through social networks and satellite television.

## Background and Organizing Groups

The April 6 Youth Movement was founded in 2008 in solidarity with Egyptian factory workers who had called a general strike in the textile town of Mahalla al-Kubra on April 6 of that year. By 2011 it had developed significant online organizing capacity. Ahead of the January 25 protests, the movement distributed an estimated 20,000 leaflets calling on Egyptians to demonstrate for their rights, while co-founder Asmaa Mahfouz posted a widely shared YouTube video urging citizens not to fear the government and to gather in Tahrir Square.

Wael Ghonim, a Google marketing executive, had anonymously created the Facebook page "We Are All Khaled Said" in June 2010 to publicize the death of Khaled Said, a 28-year-old Egyptian man beaten to death by police in Alexandria. The page attracted hundreds of thousands of followers and became a central forum for documenting police abuse. On January 28 — the "Friday of Anger" — Ghonim was arrested by plainclothes security agents and held in secret detention for 11 days. After his release, his emotional television interview in which he confirmed his role as the page's administrator and wept over the deaths of protesters is widely credited with having reinvigorated the protest movement during its most difficult period.

## Key Events

The protests began on January 25 with an estimated 50,000 or more people occupying Tahrir Square and adjacent streets. Over the following days demonstrations grew dramatically: by January 31 correspondents reported at least 250,000 in Tahrir Square alone, and by February 1 Al Jazeera estimated more than one million protesters gathered there and in surrounding streets.

The most violent episode of the uprising occurred on February 2 in what became known as the **Battle of the Camel** (Arabic: _Mawqat al-Jamal_). Pro-Mubarak supporters, some riding camels and horses and brandishing sticks, rocks, and Molotov cocktails, charged into Tahrir Square in coordinated attacks on anti-government demonstrators. The assault resulted in at least three deaths and an estimated 600 injuries. The attackers were widely understood to include members of the security services and hired thugs. International condemnation was swift, and the spectacle appeared to harden resolve among the protesters and galvanized international opinion against Mubarak.

## Mubarak's Resignation

On February 10, Mubarak delivered a televised address that many demonstrators had expected to announce his resignation; instead he announced only a delegation of some powers to Vice President Omar Suleiman. The speech drew mass anger in Tahrir Square. The following evening, February 11, 2011, Omar Suleiman appeared on state television at approximately 6:00 p.m. local time to announce: "President Hosni Mubarak has decided to leave the position of the president of the republic and has charged the high council of the armed forces to administer the affairs of the country." The announcement was greeted with massive celebrations across Egypt. SCAF, the body of senior military commanders, assumed executive authority and governed the country through a transitional period. Parliamentary elections were held in late 2011 and early 2012, with the Muslim Brotherhood's Freedom and Justice Party winning a plurality. Mohamed Morsi of the Muslim Brotherhood won the June 2012 presidential election but was removed by a military coup in July 2013.

## Significance

The Egyptian Revolution was one of the defining events of the Arab Spring — the wave of pro-democracy uprisings that swept the Middle East and North Africa in 2011 — and demonstrated that mass popular mobilization could topple a longstanding authoritarian government supported by substantial security apparatus and regional powers. The fall of Mubarak reverberated across the region, intensifying protests in Libya, Bahrain, Yemen, and Syria that were already underway or about to begin.

The revolution also brought global attention to the organizing power of social media in authoritarian contexts, particularly Facebook, Twitter, and satellite television through Al Jazeera, though scholars have since debated the relative weight of digital tools versus deep structural grievances and pre-existing activist networks. Wael Ghonim's account of the uprising, published in his 2012 memoir *Revolution 2.0*, crystallized the narrative of a networked, leaderless revolution. The longer-term outcome for Egypt was democratic reversal: the military coup of 2013 and the subsequent presidency of Abdel Fattah el-Sisi returned Egypt to authoritarian governance, leaving the revolution's democratic aspirations unfulfilled.
