# Yemeni Revolution (2011)

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: President Ali Abdullah Saleh, Vice President Abd Rabbuh Mansur Hadi, General Ali Mohsen al-Ahmar, activist Tawakkol Karman, the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC)

## Summary

The Yemeni Revolution was a sustained popular uprising against President Ali Abdullah Saleh that unfolded across 2011 as part of the wider Arab Spring. Inspired by the revolts in Tunisia and Egypt, mass demonstrations began in late January 2011 and grew into an open-ended sit-in movement demanding an end to Saleh's rule of more than three decades, along with reforms to address poverty, corruption, and unemployment in the Arab world's poorest country.

Unlike the swift transitions in Tunisia and Egypt, Yemen's uprising became a protracted, often violent stalemate that fractured the security forces, drew in tribal militias, and pushed the country toward armed confrontation. It ended only when Saleh, weakened by an assassination attempt, signed a Gulf Cooperation Council–brokered transition deal in November 2011 and formally ceded power in early 2012.

## Origins and Escalation

Protests gathered pace in late January 2011, with demonstrators occupying a plaza near Sanaa University that became known as "Change Square" (Sahat al-Taghyir), and a parallel encampment in the southern city of Taiz. Saleh, who had governed North Yemen since 1978 and unified Yemen since 1990, initially offered concessions — pledging not to seek re-election or to pass power to his son — but the demonstrations only widened.

The turning point came on March 18, 2011, when government-allied gunmen opened fire on protesters in Sanaa in an incident often called the "Friday of Dignity," killing roughly 45 to 52 people. The massacre triggered a wave of high-level defections: Major General Ali Mohsen al-Ahmar, a powerful army commander, announced his support for the protesters and deployed troops to protect them, splitting the military and raising the specter of civil war.

## The GCC Initiative and the Palace Bombing

The six-nation Gulf Cooperation Council put forward a transition plan under which Saleh would hand power to his vice president in exchange for immunity from prosecution. Saleh repeatedly signaled acceptance, then balked; he refused to sign the accord on three occasions during the spring of 2011, prolonging the crisis.

On June 3, 2011, a bomb exploded in the mosque of the presidential compound in Sanaa, leaving Saleh with severe burns and shrapnel wounds. He was evacuated to Saudi Arabia for months of medical treatment, leaving Vice President Abd Rabbuh Mansur Hadi as acting head of state. Saleh's return to Yemen in September reignited fighting between loyalist forces and opposition tribal and military factions.

## Transition and Aftermath

On November 23, 2011, Saleh flew to Riyadh and signed the GCC transition agreement he had long resisted, agreeing to transfer authority to Hadi within 30 days. Saleh formally stepped down and Hadi was confirmed as president through a single-candidate election held on February 21, 2012, ending Saleh's 33-year rule.

The estimated death toll of the uprising ran into the low thousands. The transition it produced proved fragile: unlike other Arab Spring states, Yemen negotiated a managed handover that left much of the old power structure intact, and the unresolved grievances helped set the stage for the Houthi takeover of Sanaa and the devastating civil war that followed from 2014. Internationally, the uprising was recognized in October 2011 when Yemeni journalist and activist Tawakkol Karman, a prominent voice of the protest movement, shared the Nobel Peace Prize.
