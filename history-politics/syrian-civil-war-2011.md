# Onset of the Syrian Civil War (2011)

**Category**: History & Politics  
**Key figures**: Bashar al-Assad (President of Syria), Riad al-Asaad (founder, Free Syrian Army), Mouawiya Syasneh (one of the Daraa graffiti students whose arrest ignited protests)

## Summary

In 2011, Syria experienced a violent rupture that transformed a wave of popular pro-democracy protests into an armed uprising and, ultimately, a prolonged civil war. The trajectory followed the broader arc of the Arab Spring but acquired its own catastrophic scale. Initial demonstrations in March 2011 — sparked by the arrest and reported torture of teenage students in the southern city of Daraa — drew a military crackdown from President Bashar al-Assad's government rather than the political concessions that might have de-escalated the crisis. By the end of 2011, more than 5,000 people had been killed according to UN officials, the country's 48-year state of emergency had been nominally lifted, military defectors had established the Free Syrian Army, and Russia and China had vetoed a UN Security Council resolution condemning the violence.

The conflict's roots lay in decades of Ba'athist authoritarian rule, deep economic inequality, and the particular grievances of Syria's rural and peripheral populations. The drought conditions that had gripped parts of southern Syria since 2006 exacerbated unemployment and rural poverty, particularly in Daraa province, contributing to the social pressures that made the region an early flashpoint.

## The Spark: Daraa, March 2011

On March 6, 2011, approximately fifteen students — among them Mouawiya Syasneh, then about thirteen years old — were arrested in Daraa after writing anti-regime graffiti on city walls. The slogans included "الشعب يريد إسقاط النظام" ("the people want to bring down the regime" — the signature chant of the Arab Spring) and "Your turn, Doctor" — a reference to Assad's background as a trained ophthalmologist. The students were reportedly held and tortured by the Political Security branch, headed by Atef Najib, a first cousin of President Assad.

Protests demanding the release of the students, an end to corruption, and broader political freedoms erupted on March 15 — a date later designated the official start of the Syrian uprising — with demonstrations also appearing in Damascus and Aleppo. On March 18, "Friday of Dignity" protests following prayers drew thousands in Daraa; security forces opened fire, killing at least two to four demonstrators in what became the first lethal use of force against the protest movement.

The crackdown accelerated in subsequent weeks. Funeral processions for the dead drew tens of thousands of mourners, who were themselves fired upon. By late March, protests had spread to Homs, Hama, Baniyas, and Latakia. Security forces killed several dozen people across multiple cities during the "Bloody Friday" protests of April 22, 2011.

## Government Response and the Siege of Daraa

The Assad government responded to the uprising with a combination of limited announced reforms and escalating military force. On April 19–21, 2011, Assad announced the repeal of Syria's state of emergency — in force since 1963 — and the lifting of restrictions on peaceful assembly, moves the opposition dismissed as insufficient and cosmetic. On April 25, the Syrian Army launched a ten-day siege of Daraa, deploying tanks, helicopters, and an estimated 6,000 troops to seal off and pacify the city. Snipers were positioned on rooftops; water, electricity, and communications were cut. The siege of Daraa became emblematic of the regime's willingness to use overwhelming military force against civilian populations.

## Militarization and the Free Syrian Army

Through the summer of 2011, the government extended major military operations to Hama, Homs, and other cities. On July 31, security forces killed more than 100 protesters in Hama in what observers called one of the deadliest single days of the uprising. Soldiers and lower-ranking officers began defecting rather than fire on civilian crowds. On July 29, 2011, Colonel Riad al-Asaad and six fellow defectors from the Syrian Armed Forces announced the formation of the **Free Syrian Army** (FSA), declaring their intention to fight an insurgency to overthrow Assad's government. The FSA's founding date is generally recognized as the transition point between the largely nonviolent civil uprising and armed conflict.

By September 2011, fighting between defectors and loyalist forces had broken out in Rastan. Distinct armed opposition factions multiplied, and peaceful protest, while continuing in many areas, was increasingly interwoven with armed resistance.

## International Dimensions in 2011

The Assad government's crackdown generated early international condemnation, but efforts at coordinated international response were blocked. On October 4, 2011, Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council draft resolution that would have condemned "grave and systematic" human rights violations in Syria — the first of what would become a series of vetoes shielding the Assad government from binding UN action. By October 4, approximately 2,700 Syrians had been killed.

On November 12, 2011, the Arab League voted to suspend Syria's membership and impose sanctions, an unusual step for the body. The League also proposed an Arab Initiative calling for an immediate halt to violence and national dialogue. Facing further League pressure, Assad agreed in December 2011 to allow Arab League monitors into the country. A mission of Arab observers arrived on December 26, 2011, but suspended operations on January 28, 2012, citing escalating violence.

## Significance

The 2011 Syrian uprising was among the most consequential events of the Arab Spring and of the broader post-2001 period. What began as nonviolent protest against an authoritarian government became, over the following years, one of the worst humanitarian catastrophes of the twenty-first century. By 2021, the UN estimated total deaths at over 350,000 directly from violence, with some monitoring groups placing the figure above 500,000; approximately 6.6 million Syrians became external refugees and 6.7 million were internally displaced. The conflict drew in regional powers (Turkey, Iran, Saudi Arabia, Qatar) and global ones (Russia, the United States), reshaped the political geography of the Middle East, and drove the largest refugee crisis in Europe since the Second World War.

The Assad government's fall came not in 2011 but in December 2024, when a rapid rebel offensive led by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham captured Damascus. The thirteen-year trajectory from the Daraa graffiti arrests to that outcome made the 2011 uprising one of the defining origin points of the contemporary international order.
