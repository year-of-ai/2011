# Music in 2011

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Adele, Beyoncé, Drake, Arcade Fire, Amy Winehouse, Kanye West, Jay-Z, Katy Perry, LMFAO, Rihanna

## Summary

The year 2011 marked a transformative moment in popular music, characterized by the dominance of album-oriented releases, the mainstream acceleration of hip-hop and electronic influences, and the rise of female artists. Adele's sophomore album *21*, released on February 22, 2011, became a global commercial and critical phenomenon — it sold over 31 million copies worldwide, making it the best-selling album of the 21st century at the time. At the 53rd Grammy Awards ceremony (February 13, 2011), Arcade Fire's *The Suburbs* won Album of the Year, a significant validation of indie-rock credibility at the industry's highest stage; one year later, at the 54th ceremony (February 12, 2012), Adele's *21* swept every category for which it was nominated.

Drake's *Take Care* (released November 15, 2011) debuted with 631,000 copies sold in its first week in the United States, signaling his breakthrough as a mainstream force blending R&B sensibility with hip-hop production. Meanwhile, Kanye West and Jay-Z's collaborative album *Watch the Throne* (August 2011) sold 436,000 copies in its opening week and debuted at number one in both the US and UK. Beyoncé revealed a secret pregnancy at the MTV Video Music Awards on August 28, 2011 — a pop-cultural milestone that generated global media coverage. The year also witnessed the tragic death of Amy Winehouse on July 23, 2011, at age 27, a watershed moment that prompted global reflection on artist vulnerability and the pressures of celebrity culture.

## Key Albums and Chart Milestones

**Adele — *21***: Released February 22, 2011; spent 24 non-consecutive weeks at number one on the Billboard 200; won six Grammy Awards at the 54th ceremony, including Album of the Year, Record of the Year, and Song of the Year (for "Rolling in the Deep"). "Rolling in the Deep" spent seven weeks at number one on the Billboard Hot 100.

**LMFAO — "Party Rock Anthem"**: The most-streamed song of 2011 globally; spent 57 weeks on the Hot 100, peaking at number one in June 2011 and dominating summer radio. It exemplified the rise of electro-pop and EDM crossover into mainstream charts.

**Kanye West & Jay-Z — *Watch the Throne***: Released August 8, 2011; debuted at number one on the Billboard 200 with 436,000 copies sold; nominated for Album of the Year at the 54th Grammy Awards. The album's rollout — via timed-release streaming before physical sale — anticipated the streaming-first release strategies that would dominate the decade.

**Drake — *Take Care***: Released November 15, 2011; 631,000 first-week US sales; won Grammy for Best Rap Album. Its introspective tone and production (led by Noah "40" Shebib) established the "Toronto sound" and influenced a generation of hip-hop artists.

**Beyoncé — *4***: Released June 24, 2011; debuted at number one in the US; "Run the World (Girls)" opened to widespread critical acclaim as an anthem of pop feminism. Beyoncé's performance at the VMAs on August 28, revealing her pregnancy, became one of the most-watched live moments in MTV history.

**Arcade Fire — *The Suburbs***: Released August 2010 and eligible for the 53rd Grammy Awards (held February 13, 2011); won Album of the Year, making Arcade Fire the first band on an independent label to win that award in the modern era, stunning the industry and galvanizing independent music communities worldwide.

## Industry Trends: Streaming, Vinyl, and Digital Transition

July 2011 marked a pivotal industry milestone: Spotify launched its service in the United States on July 14, 2011, having already established a base of 1 million paying subscribers in Europe. US launch expanded Spotify's total user base to over 2 million paid subscribers by year-end, laying the groundwork for the streaming decade. iTunes and digital download sales still dominated revenue, but Spotify's US entry signaled the coming disruption.

Vinyl record sales in the United States rose to approximately 3.9 million units in 2011, the highest level since the early 1990s — a trend driven by audiophile nostalgia, indie-record-store culture, and major labels including vinyl editions in album bundles. Record Store Day (April 16, 2011) saw over 1,700 participating stores in the US.

## Amy Winehouse: July 23, 2011

Amy Winehouse, 27, was found dead at her home in Camden, London, on July 23, 2011. A coroner's inquest ruled her cause of death as accidental alcohol poisoning — her blood-alcohol level was more than five times the legal UK drink-drive limit. Her death joined a grim roster of musicians who died at 27 (the so-called "27 Club," which includes Jimi Hendrix, Janis Joplin, Jim Morrison, and Kurt Cobain), intensifying media discussion around mental health, addiction, and the music industry's obligations to artists.

Winehouse's second album *Back to Black* (2006) had sold over 20 million copies worldwide by the time of her death; it re-entered the UK Albums Chart at number one within days of her passing. Her death prompted tributes across the industry and renewed critical attention to addiction support structures for performers.

## Significance

Music in 2011 reflected and accelerated several industry-wide shifts. The dominance of Adele's *21* and Arcade Fire's Grammy recognition signaled the continued power of album-oriented narratives even as streaming began reshaping music consumption. The year marked a visible shift toward female-fronted pop and R&B as dominant commercial forces. Drake's breakthrough highlighted hip-hop's increasing crossover into pop audiences. Amy Winehouse's death sparked lasting discourse about artist welfare and addiction. The prevalence of high-profile collaborations — from *Watch the Throne* to global remix culture — reflected a shift away from singular authorship. Spotify's US launch and steady vinyl growth captured a music industry caught between nostalgia and digital disruption on the eve of streaming's full dominance.
