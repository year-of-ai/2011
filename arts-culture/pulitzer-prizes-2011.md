# 2011 Pulitzer Prizes

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Jennifer Egan, Bruce Norris, Eric Foner, Ron Chernow, Siddhartha Mukherjee, Carol Guzy, the *Los Angeles Times* staff

## Summary

On April 18, 2011, the Pulitzer Prize Board announced its winners across twenty-one categories, honoring the year's finest achievements in journalism, literature, drama, history, biography, music, and photography. The prizes recognized a diverse range of cultural and intellectual contributions, with particular emphasis on American themes and historical examination. Jennifer Egan's experimental novel *A Visit From the Goon Squad* won the Fiction prize for its "inventive investigation of growing up and growing old in the digital age," while Bruce Norris's play *Clybourne Park* took Drama for its exploration of race and class in contemporary America.

The 2011 awards reflected a broad intellectual landscape spanning American history, science, and journalism. Eric Foner's *The Fiery Trial: Abraham Lincoln and American Slavery* received the History prize, Ron Chernow won Biography for his authoritative *Washington: A Life*, and Siddhartha Mukherjee's *The Emperor of All Maladies: A Biography of Cancer* captured General Nonfiction by presenting the disease's cultural and medical history. In photography, Carol Guzy of *The Washington Post* made history as the first journalist to win four Pulitzer Prizes, taking the Breaking News Photography category.

## Book and Arts Awards

### Fiction

Jennifer Egan's *A Visit From the Goon Squad* (Knopf, 2010) won the Fiction prize with a citation praising "its electric charge of invention." The novel unfolds across thirteen chapters, each told from a different point of view and in a different style — including one chapter narrated entirely through a 75-slide PowerPoint presentation created by an 11-year-old character. Egan structured the book as a non-linear exploration of time, music, and memory, centering on the New York punk music scene from the 1970s to a near-future 2020s. The novel had already won the National Book Critics Circle Award for Fiction in March 2011, making the Pulitzer a confirmation of its literary standing.

### Drama

Bruce Norris's *Clybourne Park* received the Drama prize, extending the reach of Lorraine Hansberry's 1959 landmark *A Raisin in the Sun* into a two-act examination of neighborhood racial transformation. Act One is set in 1959 Chicago, dramatizing the white family who sold their home to the Youngers of Hansberry's original play; Act Two is set in 2009 in the same house, now in a gentrifying black neighborhood. The Steppenwolf Theatre Company in Chicago premiered the play in 2010 before it transferred to the Royal Court Theatre in London and then to Broadway, where it ran at the Walter Kerr Theatre beginning in April 2012. Norris won the Olivier Award for Best New Play in 2011 for the London production.

### History

Eric Foner's *The Fiery Trial: Abraham Lincoln and American Slavery* (W. W. Norton, 2010) won History. Foner, DeWitt Clinton Professor of History at Columbia University, examined how Lincoln's views on slavery evolved across his political career — from a cautious border-state pragmatist to the president who issued the Emancipation Proclamation. The book drew on Lincoln's speeches, letters, and contemporaneous records to argue that Lincoln's trajectory was neither inevitable nor inconsistent, but rather a product of deliberate moral and political engagement. Foner was already a recipient of a Bancroft Prize (1989) and the Lincoln Prize (2011) for the same book, making his Pulitzer a capstone to a year of recognition.

### Biography or Autobiography

Ron Chernow's *Washington: A Life* (Penguin Press, 2010) won Biography. Chernow, whose previous biographies of Alexander Hamilton and John D. Rockefeller had set new standards for narrative nonfiction, produced a 904-page account of George Washington that went beyond heroic myth to examine Washington's complex relationships with slavery — 317 enslaved people lived at Mount Vernon at the time of his death, of whom he owned 123 outright — his management of wartime logistics, and his deliberate cultivation of the presidency as an institution. Chernow had previously won the Pulitzer for Biography in 1998 for *Titan: The Life of John D. Rockefeller, Sr.*, making Washington his second Pulitzer.

### General Nonfiction

Siddhartha Mukherjee's *The Emperor of All Maladies: A Biography of Cancer* (Scribner, 2010) won General Nonfiction. Mukherjee, an oncologist and researcher at Columbia University Medical Center, wrote a history of cancer — "a biography of one of the most tenacious and resilient diseases known to medicine" — from its earliest recorded appearances in ancient Egypt through the twentieth-century development of chemotherapy, radiation therapy, and targeted drug therapies. The book was described by Pulitzer judges as "an elegant inquiry." It was later adapted into a three-part PBS documentary film by Ken Burns in 2015.

### Music

Zhou Long's opera *Madame White Snake*, commissioned by Opera Boston and the Beijing Music Festival, won the Music prize. Zhou Long, born in Beijing in 1953 and educated at Columbia University, based the opera on the classical Chinese legend of Bai Suzhen, a white snake spirit who transforms into a human woman. Opera Boston premiered the work on February 26, 2010, at the Cutler Majestic Theatre. The Pulitzer Board praised it as "a deeply expressive opera that draws on a Chinese folk tale to blend the musical traditions of the East and West."

## Journalism Awards

### Public Service

The *Los Angeles Times* won the Public Service prize — the Pulitzer's highest honor — for its investigation into the City of Bell, California, a small working-class municipality where city officials had secretly awarded themselves salaries of up to $800,000 per year. Reporters Ruben Vives and Jeff Gottlieb broke the story in July 2010, prompting the arrest of eight current and former officials, including City Manager Robert Rizzo, who had been earning $787,637 annually. The investigation demonstrated investigative local journalism's capacity to hold municipal government accountable and triggered statewide legislation governing executive compensation in California cities.

### Breaking News Photography

Carol Guzy of *The Washington Post* became the first journalist to win four Pulitzer Prizes. She had previously won in 1986 (Spot News Photography, as part of a two-member team covering the Armero mudslide in Colombia), 1995 (Spot News Photography, for coverage of the U.S. military intervention in Haiti), and 2000 (Feature Photography, for coverage of Kosovar refugees). Her 2011 prize, in the Breaking News Photography category, recognized her photographs of survivors of the January 2010 Haiti earthquake.

### National Reporting

Jesse Eisinger and Jake Bernstein of ProPublica won the National Reporting prize for their series "The Wall Street Money Machine," examining how Wall Street bankers created and sold complex mortgage-backed securities that they internally expected to fail — and in some cases bet against — while marketing them to clients as sound investments. The reporting illuminated the financial engineering that worsened the 2008 financial crisis. It was ProPublica's second Pulitzer — the nonprofit newsroom, founded in 2007, had won its first the previous year — and the first Pulitzer awarded to a body of work that never appeared in print, a milestone for digital and nonprofit journalism.

## Significance

The 2011 Pulitzer Prizes reflected American cultural preoccupations with digital transformation, historical reckoning, and investigative journalism. Jennifer Egan's fiction win represented a milestone for experimental, formally innovative literature — her novel pioneered new narrative structures, including sections told through PowerPoint slides, demonstrating literature's evolution in the digital age. The awards also underscored journalism's commitment to accountability: the *Los Angeles Times* won Public Service for its investigation into municipal corruption in Bell, California, while ProPublica's National Reporting prize validated the emerging model of nonprofit digital journalism. Carol Guzy's historic fourth prize acknowledged visual storytelling's power in documenting human crisis. Mukherjee's cancer biography and Chernow's Washington biography together reflected sustained public appetite for deeply researched long-form nonfiction addressing American history and science.
