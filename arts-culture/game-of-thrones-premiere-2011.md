# Game of Thrones Series Premiere (April 17, 2011)

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: George R. R. Martin (source author), David Benioff (creator/showrunner), D. B. Weiss (creator/showrunner), Tim Van Patten (pilot director), Peter Dinklage (Tyrion Lannister), Emilia Clarke (Daenerys Targaryen), Sean Bean (Eddard Stark)

## Summary

On April 17, 2011, HBO broadcast the first episode of *Game of Thrones*, the epic fantasy television series adapted from George R. R. Martin's novel sequence *A Song of Ice and Fire*. The pilot episode, titled "Winter Is Coming," was written by showrunners David Benioff and D. B. Weiss and directed by Tim Van Patten, and ran for approximately 62 minutes. It attracted 2.2 million U.S. viewers on its initial airing, with an additional roughly 2 million watching same-night repeats. The premiere launched what would become one of the most-watched and culturally influential drama series in television history, transforming HBO's prestige portfolio and demonstrating the commercial viability of large-scale fantasy as a mainstream television genre.

The series is adapted from Martin's *A Song of Ice and Fire* saga — a multi-volume fantasy epic beginning with *A Game of Thrones* (1996) — set in the fictional continents of Westeros and Essos, where noble houses compete for the Iron Throne while an ancient supernatural threat stirs beyond a vast northern wall. Benioff and Weiss had first approached Martin in 2006 about a television adaptation; HBO ordered the pilot in 2008 and the full series in 2010 after the pilot was reshot. The original pilot — directed by Tom McCarthy and filmed in late 2009 — was judged unsatisfactory by HBO, and approximately 90 percent of it was reshot under Van Patten in 2010 before the network committed to a full first season.

## First Season and Critical Reception

The first season comprised ten episodes, concluding on June 19, 2011. Viewership grew steadily across the season, rising approximately 33% from the premiere's 2.2 million to over 3 million viewers for the finale, with total viewership (including delayed viewing and repeat airings) considerably higher. HBO renewed the series for a second season just two days after the pilot aired, reflecting strong executive confidence in its trajectory.

Critical reception for the first season was strongly positive. On Rotten Tomatoes, the season earned a 90% approval rating from 224 critics, with an average score of 8.35 out of 10; the site's consensus praised "intricate storytelling and dark themes" accessible "even to non-genre fans." The season received 13 Emmy Award nominations, winning two: Peter Dinklage for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Drama Series — for his portrayal of the acerbic, politically astute Tyrion Lannister — and Outstanding Main Title Design for Angus Wall and Lillian Barbeito's animated map sequence that became iconic in its own right. The series also received a Peabody Award for 2011. Dinklage additionally won Golden Globe, Scream Award, and Satellite Award recognition for his performance.

## Production and Adaptation

The show's production scale was unprecedented for an episodic drama, with the first season shot on location in Northern Ireland, Malta, and Iceland and involving large casts, elaborate costumes, and practical sets. The budget per episode was estimated at around $6 million — substantially higher than most contemporary television dramas. Martin's novels provided rich source material; the first season closely followed the plot of *A Game of Thrones*, ending with the death of the apparent protagonist, Eddard Stark (Sean Bean), in a climax that signaled the series' willingness to violate conventional narrative expectations.

Emilia Clarke's portrayal of exiled Targaryen queen Daenerys — including sequences involving CGI dragons and scenes of personal peril — drew particular critical attention alongside Dinklage and Bean. The ensemble cast included more than a dozen major roles, setting a template for the sprawling, multi-protagonist structure that would define the series through its eight-season run (2011–2019).

## Significance

The premiere of *Game of Thrones* in April 2011 represents a watershed in the history of prestige television and of fantasy as a mainstream commercial genre. It demonstrated that HBO, which had built its dramatic reputation on contemporary realism (*The Sopranos*, *The Wire*, *Deadwood*), could extend that prestige model into epic fantasy without sacrificing critical credibility. It also helped establish the model of large-budget narrative television that would define the streaming era: the show's eventual eighth season (2019) was watched by an average of nearly 44 million viewers per episode across all platforms in the United States alone.

The premiere coincided with — and contributed to — a broader cultural shift in the early 2010s in which fantasy and science fiction, once marginalized in mainstream cultural discourse, became dominant forms for popular storytelling. Martin's *A Song of Ice and Fire* novels had already attracted a devoted readership, but the HBO adaptation brought the work to audiences an order of magnitude larger. The series won more Emmy Awards (59) than any other drama series in history, a record set over its run through 2019 and reflecting its sustained cultural presence well beyond its 2011 debut.
