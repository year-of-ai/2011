# Death of Amy Winehouse

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Amy Winehouse (1983–2011); Mitch Winehouse (father); Blake Fielder-Civil (ex-husband); Mark Ronson (producer); Nick Shymansky (early manager)

## Summary

Amy Winehouse, the British-Jewish singer-songwriter known for her distinctive mezzo-soprano vocals and eclectic fusion of jazz, soul, and R&B, died on July 23, 2011, at the age of 27. She was found unresponsive at her home at 30 Camden Square, London, at approximately 3:54 PM by her security guard. The Westminster Coroner's Court returned a verdict of accidental death; toxicology reports indicated a blood alcohol concentration of 416 mg per 100 mL — more than five times the legal driving limit of 80 mg per 100 mL in England and Wales.

Winehouse had publicly struggled with substance abuse and personal difficulties for several years preceding her death. Her final concert performance, on June 18, 2011, at the Heineken Open'er Festival in Gdynia, Poland, was cut short after approximately 45 minutes when she appeared visibly impaired and could not continue.

## Career and Musical Legacy

Amy Jade Winehouse was born September 14, 1983, in Southgate, London, to a Jewish family. She attended the Sylvia Young Theatre School briefly and later studied at the BRIT School in Croydon, where she developed her distinctive artistic identity.

### Albums and Commercial Success

Her debut album, **"Frank"** (2003), released on Island Records, blended jazz, neo-soul, and hip-hop influences and earned her Ivor Novello Award nominations. While it was a critical and moderate commercial success in the UK, it was her second album that transformed her into a global phenomenon.

**"Back to Black"** (released October 27, 2006) became one of the best-selling albums in British chart history, spending three consecutive weeks at No. 1 in the UK. The album was produced by Mark Ronson and Salaam Remi and drew heavily on 1960s girl-group sounds, Motown soul, and jazz. Key singles included "Rehab," "You Know I'm No Good," "Back to Black," and "Tears Dry on Their Own." "Back to Black" sold more than 20 million copies worldwide.

### Grammy Awards

At the 50th Grammy Awards on February 10, 2008, Winehouse won five Grammy Awards — a record for a British female artist at the time — including:
- **Record of the Year** ("Rehab")
- **Song of the Year** ("Rehab")
- **Best New Artist**
- **Best Female Pop Vocal Performance** ("Rehab")
- **Best Female R&B Vocal Performance** ("Love Is a Losing Game")

She accepted her awards via a satellite link from London, as US authorities had initially denied her visa application due to drug-related arrests. Her performance — visually distinctive in her signature beehive hairstyle and retro dress — became one of the most-watched Grammy moments of the decade.

### Artistic Influence

Winehouse's fusion of retro aesthetics with contemporary production strongly influenced the sound of British and American popular music in the late 2000s and 2010s. Artists including Adele, Duffy, Lana Del Rey, and Florence Welch acknowledged her influence on their own approaches to vintage-inflected pop and soul. Her label, Island Records, signed several acts in her wake whose success owed a debt to her commercial breakthrough of jazz-inflected British soul.

## The 27 Club

Winehouse's death at age 27 placed her alongside a group of celebrated musicians who died at the same age: Robert Johnson, Brian Jones, Jimi Hendrix, Janis Joplin, Jim Morrison, and Kurt Cobain. The "27 Club" is not a formal or statistically meaningful category — musicologists have noted that mortality risk among rock musicians does not spike unusually at 27 — but the cultural coincidence generated significant media and public reflection on the pressures of early fame, mental health, and addiction in the music industry.

## Response and Legacy

Winehouse's death generated an outpouring of grief worldwide. In the 24 hours following her death, "Back to Black" re-entered the UK charts, climbing to No. 1 — making Winehouse the first artist in UK chart history to have an album re-enter at No. 1 following the artist's death. Her albums collectively re-entered charts in over 20 countries.

A BBC tribute documentary aired within weeks, and a foundation — the Amy Winehouse Foundation — was established by her family in October 2011 to support young people struggling with drug and alcohol dependency and to provide music education opportunities.

The 2015 documentary **"Amy"**, directed by Asif Kapadia, won the Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature in 2016. The film drew critical acclaim for its candid footage and its examination of the role of the media, management, and personal relationships in her decline.

A biographical film, "Back to Black" (2024), directed by Sam Taylor-Johnson and starring Marisa Abela, brought renewed interest to Winehouse's life story, though it received mixed critical reviews.

## Significance

Winehouse's death prompted broader public and industry discussion about mental health, addiction, and the responsibilities of the music industry toward artists. Her case illustrated how tabloid media culture, commercially driven management, and the lack of robust mental health infrastructure within the industry can accelerate the deterioration of artists in crisis.

Her musical legacy endures as one of the most distinctive British voices of the 2000s. The year 2011 saw the music world also grapple with the deaths of other iconic figures; her passing was one of the most viscerally felt losses in a year that saw considerable cultural mourning across the arts. The continued commercial performance of "Back to Black" — which by 2023 had sold over 20 million copies — testifies to the enduring resonance of her work.
